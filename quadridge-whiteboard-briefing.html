Use Cases

Three distinct problem areas surfaced on the discovery call. All three are genuine. The question for the whiteboard is which one becomes the entry point — and David (Quadridge) already told us: start small, move fast, phase up.

Priority · Use Case 01

Legal Document Analysis & Board Pack Intelligence

Every time a new structured finance transaction lands, Quadridge receives 600–800 pages of legal documentation. They have to read it, understand the structure, identify the obligations, and sometimes comment on the legals. David's word: "you just cannot actually go through all of that detail, so we just literally skim." Board packs are the same problem — hundreds of pages, and the team needs to spot red flags in financial statements before meetings.

The opportunity

Document ingestion, extraction of key obligations and covenants, summarisation of financial data with source referencing, and red-flag highlighting on financial statements. This is the closest match to the asset management case study Jeremy walked through on the discovery call — where the AI extracts the seven things you have to monitor from a 500-page lending document and tells you exactly where it found each data point.

Why this is the entry point

It's the most painful (they're literally skimming 800-page documents), the most familiar to us (we've built this), and the most demonstrable in a short timeframe. David also mentioned wanting "high level summaries" of legal documentation as "a huge boon." The language of relief was strongest here.

Secondary · Use Case 02

Covenant Monitoring & Compliance Oversight

Quadridge provides oversight on structured finance transactions — checking that data from the asset manager or issuer matches the covenants, that cash flows reconcile with bank statements, and that nothing is in breach. This is currently done in Excel spreadsheets, manually, and "subject to human error." The banks are increasingly pushing this work onto Quadridge rather than doing it in-house, which means the volume is growing while the team isn't.

The opportunity

Automated covenant extraction from transaction documents, ongoing monitoring against reported data, breach detection and amber/red flagging, and a dashboard view similar to what David Carter described on the call — "100 companies have submitted reports, these three are in amber, this one has gone below cash at the bank." Source referencing throughout so analysts can verify every flag.

Why secondary, not primary

This is a bigger build. It requires understanding their specific data flows, integration with however they currently receive reporting data, and the monitoring logic is transaction-specific. It's a Phase 2 conversation once we've proven ourselves on document analysis. But it's the long-term value play — this is where the real operational leverage sits.

Exploratory · Use Case 03

Workflow Automation — Entity Setup & Administration

Setting up companies, trusts, and SPVs follows a predetermined process every time — same forms, same engagements, same data entry. Tax returns have different deadlines across entities, and David admitted that "it can be easy for one or two to slip through the cracks." Meeting deadlines, timetables — all manually tracked.

The opportunity

Process automation for entity setup workflows, intelligent deadline tracking and alerting across the portfolio, and template-driven document generation. This is more traditional process automation than AI — but with an AI layer for exception handling and intelligent scheduling.

Why exploratory

This is real and it's painful, but it's further from our core strength (which is the language and document intelligence layer) and closer to traditional process automation / workflow tooling. We can address it, but it shouldn't be the thing that defines the relationship. Park it as a Phase 3 conversation and keep the focus on where AI creates step-change value.

Whiteboard Playbook

This is a 90-minute session. The discovery call already covered a lot of ground — they told us their problems, we showed relevant case studies, and both sides liked what they heard. The whiteboard needs to go deeper, not wider.

Opening — First 10 Minutes ▼

Don't re-pitch. They already had an hour with David Carter and Jeremy. The opening should be: "Thanks for making time for this. Last session you walked us through three areas — document analysis, covenant monitoring, and workflow automation. Before we dig into any of those, has anything changed since we last spoke? Any new priorities, or anything that's moved up the list?"

This does two things: it shows we retained what they said (which matters to a small firm — they're used to being forgotten by vendors), and it gives them a chance to redirect if something has shifted. If they brought operational staff, ask those people to introduce themselves and describe what they actually do day-to-day. That's gold.

The Questions We Need Answered ▼

The discovery call gave us the "what." The whiteboard needs the "how." These are the gaps:

  • On legal document analysis: When a new transaction lands — walk me through it. Who receives the 600 pages? What do they do first? What are they actually looking for? How long does it take before the team feels confident they understand the structure? What gets missed?
  • On board packs: How far in advance do you receive them? What's the prep process before a board meeting? Where do the red flags usually hide — in the financials, the narrative, or in what's missing?
  • On covenant monitoring: How does the data come in — email, portal, spreadsheet? What does the checking process look like today? How often do breaches get caught late? What happens when one slips through?
  • On deadlines and compliance: How are tax deadlines and filing dates currently tracked? Is it a shared system or individual knowledge? When things slip, what's the consequence?
  • On technology: What systems are you running today? Email client, document management, any existing tools for covenant tracking?
  • On appetite: David said "start with the small ones first." What does "small" mean to you — small in scope, small in cost, or fast to deliver?
What to Show — And What Not To ▼

Show: The asset management case study again — but this time go deeper. On the discovery call, Jeremy set the scene and David Carter described the dashboard. In the whiteboard, make it concrete: show how the 500-page document becomes seven monitored covenants, how the dashboard surfaces amber and red flags, how the source referencing works. This is the proof point that lands hardest because it's a direct mirror of their covenant monitoring work.

Show: The private equity document processing example Jeremy mentioned — a thousand proposals distilled into 450 investment committee templates. This mirrors Quadridge's legal document analysis challenge: unstructured input, structured output, expensive humans freed to make decisions rather than move data.

Don't show: Anything that looks like a generic product demo. They explicitly said they value bespoke solutions — Jeremy's "we build outside your environment to your client specification" clearly landed. Keep everything framed as "built for you."

Draw on the whiteboard: The before/after of their legal document analysis workflow. Today: 800 pages arrive → human skims → manual extraction → hope nothing was missed. Tomorrow: 800 pages arrive → AI extracts obligations, covenants, key terms → human reviews summary with source links → flags what needs attention. Let them correct the diagram. The corrections tell us what we got wrong.

How to Frame the Staircase ▼

David (Quadridge) practically gave us the staircase himself: "identify the small ones first, quickly implemented, and then perhaps in phases." Don't fight that instinct — build on it.

Phase Scope Dependency Timeline
Discovery Whiteboard → pre-proposal. Define the problem, agree on the first use case, scope success metrics. None from their side Now → 2 weeks
Phase 1 Legal document analysis agent. Ingest transaction documents, extract key obligations and covenants, produce structured summaries with source referencing. Built in Conquer cloud against sample documents. Sample transaction docs (anonymised if needed post-NDA) 6–8 weeks
Phase 2 Covenant monitoring dashboard. Automated ingestion of reporting data, compliance checking against extracted covenants, amber/red flagging with audit trail. Reporting data samples, covenant definitions 6–8 weeks
Phase 3 Workflow automation. Entity setup, deadline tracking, board pack preparation support. Process documentation, system access TBD

The key message: Phase 1 is self-contained, low-dependency, and delivers value quickly. It also builds the foundation (document intelligence layer) that Phase 2 and Phase 3 sit on top of.

How to Close the Session ▼

The close should be simple: "Here's what we heard today. We'll turn this into a pre-proposal within two weeks — a clear problem statement, a proposed solution, a timeline, and a cost. We'll send it over and you tell us where we've got it wrong."

Then the practical: "To make the pre-proposal as specific as possible, it would help to get a couple of sample documents — even heavily redacted ones — so we can show you exactly how the AI would process them. And we should get the NDA signed so both sides can speak freely." David Carter already raised the NDA on the discovery call, so this should be straightforward.

If they push on pricing, don't dodge it. Give a range: "Phase 1 builds like this typically sit in the £X–Y range depending on complexity. We'll be precise in the pre-proposal." Don't let pricing ambiguity become a reason to stall.

Watch-Outs

Things that could derail the session or the deal if we're not careful.

Don't treat them like a small account
Kurt said it directly: they're small, but they manage billion-rand structures and are connected to every major bank in South Africa. If we treat this as a minor engagement, they'll feel it. More importantly, if we deliver well, Kurt explicitly offered to introduce us to "much bigger players." This is a gateway account. Treat the whiteboard with the same rigour as a F500 session.
Don't let the conversation stay at 30,000 feet
The discovery call was strategic by design — it was an intro. The whiteboard needs to go operational. If David and Kurt show up without operational staff and want to stay at the high level, gently push: "This is really helpful context. To make the pre-proposal sharp, can we walk through one specific example? Pick the last transaction that landed on your desk — what happened step by step?" Get into the weeds or we leave without enough to write a credible proposal.
The NDA isn't signed yet
David Carter raised NDAs at the end of the discovery call. If they haven't been signed by the time of the whiteboard, both sides will be holding back. Chase this before the session. If it's not done, acknowledge it upfront: "We know we haven't got the NDA sorted yet — let's work with what we can today and go deeper once that's in place."
Jeremy's South Africa connection is an asset — use it
Jeremy spent years working with Standard Chartered in South Africa. He mentioned it on the discovery call and it clearly registered with Kurt and David. In the whiteboard, let Jeremy lead on the case studies and the "we understand your world" framing. His credibility in their market is genuine — it's not a sales tactic, and they'll know the difference.
Don't promise what we can't staff
South Africa is a new market for Conquer. Timezone, regulatory context, and data residency may all be factors. Don't commit to timelines or delivery models on the whiteboard that assume the same setup as a UK or US engagement. If they ask about deployment specifics, be honest: "We'll need to understand your data residency requirements as part of the proposal. We build in cloud, and we'll make sure the architecture works for your environment."
